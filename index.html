3. Understand the Hawl

The hawl is the the Zakat Year, like a fiscal year, but used to calculate your Zakat. Look back in your account history. You start calculating Zakat on the day your account was greater than the ni·π£ƒÅb. If that amount remained consistently above the ni·π£ƒÅb for one year, you must pay Zakat on your total liable wealth. To keep things simple, you can set your hawl date on the day you calculated or paid Zakat last lunar year (i.e. the 14th of Ramadan).

If you're worried your total liable wealth dropped below $318.23 sometime during the hawl, double check using a snapshot of the dates in question on the calculator below. Otherwise, continue through the calculator with your current financial snapshot, and we'll let you know if and how much you owe in Zakat!

Your investments are divided into 3 categories when you're paying zakat on them: active investments, passive investments, and the dividends you receive from both of them.

11. Active Investments üìà

A good rule of thumb for active investments is: are you planning on holding these stocks for less than 366 days? You will treat these shares the same way you would if you had them as cash in your pocket.

12. Passive Investments üìä

This section applies to the stocks or funds that you are planning on holding for more than 366 days.

To calculate the Zakat on each stock you hold, you will need to access the company's balance sheet. You can find this information on Yahoo! Finance or Morningstar. Here are the numbers to note:

C - Cash and cash equivalents

R - Receivables

I - Inventories

Shares Outstanding - Total number of shares issued by the company

For example, if we are calculating the information for Microsoft, we can find the information needed on the Yahoo! Finance page.

This is the equation we will use to calculate your Zakat Liable Amount on passive investments:: $$ z = {{{C + R + I} \over outstandingShares} * sharesOwned}.$$ But don't worry about doing the math. 14. Roth IRA üéÇ

If you are below the age of 59 and a half, you will pay this ONLY on your Roth IRA contributions, since that money is accessible to you. If you are above that age, the earnings (greater than 5 years old) are also accessible for you.. Therefore, if you are above the age of 59 and a half, you should pay on your total Roth amount (and subtract any earnings less than 5 years old).

$

15. Post-tax 401(k) withdrawals üë¥

Joe Bradford's recommended Zakat calculation is to pay Zakat only on what has your full control, or what you have withdrawn. This is treating your 401k like a passive investment. If you reach retirement age or cash out, you will pay Zakat only ONE year on the entire amount, and then EVERY year after because you will have unfettered access to the full amount. If you withdraw early, then you will pay Zakat on the NET amount you withdraw after taxes and penalties.

If these distributions are already in your checking account or another account previously listed, be sure not to double count them.

$

28. Good Debt (Owed to You) üí±

Good Debt is debt that you are owed and expect to collect. If something prevents you from doing so, like the inability of a person to pay or their refusal to pay, it becomes Bad Debt. You are liable to pay Zakat on a Bad Debt when you actually receive it.

$

üöë Expenses and Liabilites

When calculating your Zakat liable amount, you should remove the amount of money that you owe to others. In addition to debts, you can also deduct bills that are "immediately due." For example, if you pay your rent monthly, you can deduct one month of your rent. If you pay your insurance every 6 months, and your insurance payment is up immediately, you can deduct the 6 month insurance bill.

29. Debts üí≥

This can include student debts, credit card debts, and any other debts that have become due. While God is merciful and forgiving, man is not and may demand his rights from us on the Day of Judgment. Therefore, pay your debts to man first. However, if a person has a large debt that is being paid off in instalments, such as a mortgage or large credit card debt, then do not include that here, but instead look at 3(c).

$

30. This Month's Living Expenses üçî

This can include: rent/mortgage payments, medical expenses (other than your insurance premium, which is covered below), utilities, groceries, transport, upkeep, and any other miscellaneous expenses. Because your bills and expenses are considered debts owed to others, you have to pay those first. You should only deduct those expenses which are immediately due.
